W e l c o m e !

Earlier this year, I attended the annual TEDx event at Georgia Tech and one quote stuck with me: “It is better to try to change 1% of Walmart rather than open up 100 Ben and Jerry’s when it comes to sustainability”. Large companies have such a huge global influence that affecting even a small part of it can have a bigger impact on the environment than one hundred smaller initiatives. This bold statement made me think about the media artifacts we collected in my “Environmentalism & Ecocriticism” class. What role do these individual media artifacts play on a global scale? With the internet as a main distribution form, can media even be local anymore? How does media fit into global efforts for sustainability?

One of the readings from this humanities class was the first chapter of “A Vast Machine” by Paul N. Edwards where he explains the historical account of climate science as a “global knowledge infrastructure with both social and technical dimensions”. He starts out by talking about the famous phrase “Think global, act local” which stands in contrast with the concept of global players as the main focus of sustainability efforts. Media provides the platform and capacity for conversations about the environment and sustainability in different local environments. It supports the global discourse of an anthropogenic climate change through a similar infrastructure of museums, internet platforms, galleries, blogs, newspapers, and libraries. In the scientific community, weather data is used to build consensus. Media artifacts receive inspiration from specific local places of nature that often are the foundation and a guideline for the story or product. In “A Vast Machine”, the author quotes Marshall McLuhan who described the “‘global village,’ the shrinkage of space and time through printing, literacy, and mass media”. Media can show what the current Zeitgeist regards as important about nature on a social and cultural level, and can voice critique about dominating economic or political players on a global scale. We can apply Edwards’ idea of “nature as data” to our environment themed media artifacts by looking at the places covered in the artifact which often correlate with the location of the audience. This location data can then be analyzed to see on what scale of audience the media artifact reaches and if the data can be replaced, so the artifact can work in different locations which would expand its reach.

This digital curatorial project aims to highlight the local context of ten different media artifacts and questions whether the projects can be adopted in different environments and whether they could benefit from a wider audience. In the spirit of catchy slogans, this EcoExhibit theme is: “Solve locally, adapt globally”. Climate Change is a pressing global issue and is so urgent that media has to provide dialogues of all scales on all levels of society are needed.

"The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn" by Mark Twain

The novel "The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn" takes place along a stretch of the Mississippi River which is an area that Mark Twain knew well. It includes his home town of Hannibal and various other well known cities down the river such as New Orleans, and St. Louis. The river serves as an ongoing plot device because it is the escape route that gets the characters out of trouble many times, and moves them on to new locations. This book stil has a global audience - it has been translated into over 50 languages and around 700 editions have been published worldwide. The local places are certainly important for this novel, but Mark Twain describes their most important features in detail and even adds some fictional cities as well, which allows for an international audience.

Erin Brockovich

The citizen of Hinkley, a small rural town in Southern California, have unusually high cancer rates and PG&E, a significant presence in the community, is paying for their health care costs. When Erin Brockovich discovers evidence for the relationship between the increased illness rates and the water quality close to the PG&E plant, she helps to file a direct-action lawsuit which ends up receiving the largest settlement ever paid in U.S. history. This local story inspired an even more global audience when it was turned into a movie starring Julia Roberts which won multiple Oscars.

Biketastic

In Biketastic, cyclists are enrolled to collect data concerning road surface conditions in Los Angeles, using the built-in accelerometers and GPS services in mobile phones. It does not have features to encourage bike use over other transportation methods, but with collecting bad road conditions, it helps to decrease the impact of potholes on cars and helps to identify the most pressing damages. Like most map-based applications, it will provide a better experience for the user in particular curated cities. This bike tracking app currently works only in Los Angeles but there are many similar ones in other places.

Serendipitor App by Mark Sheppard

Serendipitor is a navigation tool that includes detours and prompts in the route, so the user can experience the city and its nature in a new way. The app is currently only available in Rotterdam, Netherlands. There could be an algorithm that allows us to use this on a global scale but then its local charm and character might get lost. This example should rather be adopted into other smaller city-wide initiatives and scale incrementally.

Spore 1.1

Spore 1.1 is a self-containted ecosystem using a plant from Home Depot and hard that only allows watering if the stock price of Home Depot goes up. If the stock price stays the same or drops, the plant receives no water. This type of art installations let visitors engage with environmental issues in a playful, and provocative way. It was exhibited in Madrid and Dublin, but would work anywhere where a Home Depot or similar shop is located, provided that its stock is publicly traded.

Cloud at Strange Weather

This art installation, called Cloud, takes data from the last ten years of the Irish housing market and visualizes it through a series of house-shaped clouds in varying size and frequency as a way of visualizing the rise and catastrophic fall of the Irish housing market. This project was exhibited in Dublin, Copehagen, and Detroit, and would work in other places where housing data from the last ten years show interesting trends.

Satellite Imagery

This is part of the slides on Iraq that U.S. Secretary of State Colin Powell's presented to the United Nations Security Council on February 5, 2003. For many of the annotated satellite images displayed that day, we don't have access to the original satellite data. They claimed to have proof of Iraq's possession of mass destruction weapons which we know today to be false. Today, satellite images are globally accessible using technology like Google Maps and Google Earth. However, the company decides on which pictures to publish and which satellite systems to buy from.

Beltline

The Atlanta BeltLine project is based on a master’s thesis by Georgia Tech student Ryan Gravel who wanted to create a new form of transportation for Atlanta. The project will eventually connect 45 neighborhoods via trails, a streetcar and parks, and is set to complete in 2030. This ambitious project has received worldwide attention and praise, and has been called “the most important tail-transit project t hat’s been proposed in the country, possibly in the world”. Projects like this one can defintely be replicated in other cities, like the High Line in New York City, but we should also learn from the mistakes and the problems big ambitious project like this causes.

Papatya Houses

These residential houses in Turkey have green roofs and were build as a start of a community. This project does not only have impact on the residents living in it, but also the whole neighborhood around it. The architects are also part of a growing design community interested in sustainable building and growing their own market by publishing about projects like this.

M E D I A S C A P E S

Miami Design District

Blue glass façade by Sou Fujimoto is inspired by the flowing movement of waterfalls and alludes to the prevalent aquatic nature of the city. One the one hand, it provides privacy and shade for the luxurious pedestrian promenade, and, on the other hand, it allows a view on the palm trees and the main art sculpture of the outdoor courtyard, the “fly’s eye” dome. This building is located in the Miami Design District where you can find a variety of fashion and jewelry stores as well as cafes and a food court. It encompasses the Palm Court area that display different art sculptures. Specifically, the blue-tinted glass fins bring an interesting visual spectacle to the area. Tourists from all over the world come to visit Miami. It also has been a concern for environmental consequences as it will be one of the first major cities in the US to be drowned in the rising sea levels which the city already has to battle today.
